From Accurate Measurement to Behavior That Sticks:
The Engagement Gap in Continuous Blood Pressure Monitoring

Hilo has solved the hardest part — clinical-grade, cuffless, continuous BP measurement validated through trials and CE Marked. The product challenge that remains is different in kind: how do you turn a stream of medically accurate data into something a user acts on, trusts, and keeps using long enough to change their cardiovascular health?

The problem nobody is talking about

Hilo's technology moat is real. The product challenge is upstream of the technology.

✅

What Hilo has already won

Clinically validated accuracy. CE Marked Class IIa medical device. Available in 12 countries. 18 years of CSEM research behind the algorithm. Hilo wins the "does it work?" argument before a user even opens the app.

⚠️

Where the value disappears

Continuous monitoring generates a stream of data most users have no framework to interpret. A reading of 138/88 at 2pm on a Tuesday means nothing without context — trend, triggers, comparison to the morning, relationship to yesterday's walk. Without that context, users check the number, feel anxious or reassured, and do nothing differently.

🔍

The hidden product problem

Retention in consumer health wearables follows a predictable curve: high engagement in week one (novelty), steep drop-off by week six (the data makes no sense), plateau at a small engaged segment. Hilo's mission — changing cardiovascular health outcomes — requires behavior change. Behavior change requires sustained engagement. Sustained engagement requires the user to feel that the product understands them.

💡

What the PM role can fix

The engagement gap is not a data science problem. The algorithm is already good. It is a product problem — specifically a personalization, context, and intervention design problem. A PM who treats engagement retention as a first-class metric, and who knows how to run structured experiments on a health platform, can measurably close this gap within 12 months.

Why this problem, why now: Hilo is available in 12 countries and has clinical validation. The scale challenge is no longer "does it measure accurately?" — it is "does it change behavior at scale?" That is a product execution challenge, not a science one. The Senior PM role is positioned exactly to own it.

What I would learn before writing a spec

Three discovery workstreams I would run in the first 60 days — before touching the roadmap.

👤

Ride-along with users at the 6-week mark

Not user interviews. Observation sessions with users who are 5-7 weeks in — the exact point the drop-off curve steepens. What do they check? What do they ignore? What question do they ask themselves when they look at their BP trend that the app currently cannot answer? That question is the product gap.

📊

Amplitude deep-dive: engagement segmentation

Retention curves will not be uniform. There will be a cohort of users who sustain engagement at 90 days. Understanding what they do differently in weeks two and three — which features they use, what notifications they respond to, how often they add context — is more valuable than any survey. I would build this analysis before writing a single spec.

🏥

Clinical pathway mapping

Many Hilo users will be managing hypertension alongside a GP or cardiologist. Understanding how the Hilo data intersects with clinical appointments — does a user share their trend? Does the GP ask? Is there friction? — reveals a product surface that could significantly increase perceived value and retention without a single new feature.

Tensions I expect to find

Hypotheses I would test, not assumptions I would build on.

😰

Anxiety vs. insight

For some users, seeing a continuous BP trend increases health anxiety without increasing understanding. The product needs to distinguish between data that empowers and data that overwhelms — and serve them differently.

🔔

Notification fatigue

The right cadence of insights is not daily. It may not even be weekly. A user who gets an "elevated BP this afternoon" push every three days will mute the app. A user who gets one genuinely surprising, contextualized insight per week might share it with their doctor.

🧠

The calibration trust problem

Continuous monitoring surfaces variance. Users who check traditional cuffs once a week never see the intraday swings. Seeing them for the first time is alarming. The product needs to actively teach what normal variance looks like — or engagement drops when a user sees a spike and concludes the device is broken.

Product approach — three phases

Structured to generate learning at each stage before committing to the next.

1
Contextual intelligence layer — make the data mean something
Weeks 1–10

The first version does not add features. It adds context to what already exists. The goal is to make every BP reading accompanied by at least one signal that explains it — not "your BP is 138/88" but "your BP is slightly elevated this afternoon, consistent with the pattern on your last three Mondays." The user should finish the check feeling they understand something, not just feeling informed.

  • Instrument the "what happened after checking their BP" flow in Amplitude — this is the behavior that reveals whether context is working
  • Build the pattern recognition layer: day-of-week trends, activity correlation, sleep correlation (if data available)
  • Define and ship a "weekly digest" — one meaningful, personalized insight per week, not a data summary
  • North Star for this phase: 7-day and 30-day active user rate vs. week-one peak (the drop-off metric)
  • Run a hold-out experiment: no weekly digest vs. weekly digest — measure retention at day 30
2
Personalized intervention design — move from insight to action
Weeks 10–20

Once context is working and engagement has stabilized, the next challenge is behavior change. The product needs to offer a user a small, credible action they can take in response to a pattern — and then close the loop on whether it worked. This is where most health apps fail: they surface insight without creating an action path. Hilo's clinical credibility makes this tractable in a way it is not for fitness trackers.

  • Design an "experiment for yourself" feature: user selects a behavior change (morning walk, reducing coffee, consistent sleep time), sets a two-week window, Hilo tracks the BP trend against it
  • Build a goal-setting layer that connects user-defined health goals to the metrics framework — not generic targets, but personalized based on their trend
  • Explore GP sharing: a one-tap summary a user can show their doctor at the next appointment (PDF or in-app view) — this surfaces value at the moment it matters most
  • Metric: % of weekly active users who complete at least one "experiment for yourself" cycle by Month 4
3
Closed-loop learning and clinical integration
Weeks 20–36

Every completed behavior experiment generates labeled data: what a user tried, how long they tried it, and what happened to their BP. At scale this is Hilo's most defensible asset — personalized, longitudinal behavioral data attached to a clinically validated BP signal. Phase 3 formalizes the feedback loop and begins to position Hilo as the clinical companion it can become.

  • Aggregate anonymized experiment outcomes to improve the recommendation model — which interventions produce measurable BP change, for which user profiles?
  • Explore HCP (healthcare provider) integration: a structured data export for GPs managing hypertension patients on Hilo
  • Build a "longitudinal view" — a user-facing timeline connecting BP trend to logged events over 6-12 months. This is the product feature that makes Hilo irreplaceable at annual check-up time.
  • Metric: 90-day retention rate vs. pre-Phase-1 baseline; GP-shared export usage rate

Metric hierarchy

Not all metrics are equal. This is the stack I would instrument and review weekly.

⭐

North Star

D30 and D90 active user rate as a percentage of D7 rate — the engagement retention ratio. This captures the drop-off curve directly. It does not matter how many users activate if 80% are gone by week six.

📈

Leading indicators

Weekly digest open rate (predicts retention 3 weeks ahead). Behavior experiment completion (predicts long-term habit formation). Notification mute rate (early warning for friction before it shows in retention).

🏥

Health outcome proxies

% of users whose 30-day BP average improves by Month 3. % of users who share a GP report. These are lagging indicators but they are the mission metrics — the ones I would put in front of leadership quarterly.

🚫

Counter-metrics

Health anxiety rate (flagged in support tickets). Notification unsubscribe rate. BP reading frequency decline. These are the signals that tell me the product is moving in the wrong direction before the retention curve does.

How I actually use AI tools

Concrete, not aspirational — this is how I work today.

Cursor + Claude Code — rapid prototyping
At Soulspiti I built the full OMS architecture as an agentic prototype before any production code was written — choosing the no-code route over custom development that was 4x the cost and 2x the timeline. At Hilo this would mean: interactive spec prototypes I can put in front of users and engineers in hours, not days. The weekly digest layout, the behavior experiment UI, the GP report format — all prototypable before a designer opens Figma.
Amplitude + AI-assisted analysis — find the signal in the noise
I use Claude to accelerate the "what should I look for?" stage of funnel analysis — generating cohort segmentation hypotheses and SQL query drafts before I go into Amplitude. This means I spend analyst time validating hypotheses, not forming them. At Coinhaven I used this workflow to identify the checkout step that was causing 18% payment failure — the fix improved success rate within two weeks of shipping.
Lovable — visual prototypes for user testing
For user research sessions I use Lovable to build functional-looking prototypes I can put in front of users the same week a hypothesis forms. At Hilo this would mean testing the weekly digest format with real users before engineering builds anything — does "your BP trend this week" or "something changed on Tuesday" perform better? I can answer that before sprint planning starts.
Make.com / n8n — workflow automation
I use no-code automation to compress internal processes that would otherwise require engineering tickets. At Soulspiti I automated 94% of order routing without writing a line of production code. At Hilo, early versions of the GP report export, the weekly digest generation, or the notification trigger logic could all be validated with no-code before a production build is justified.
AI-assisted spec writing
I use Claude to validate spec logic before sharing with engineering — specifically to find edge cases I have not considered and to check whether acceptance criteria are ambiguous. The goal is specs that eliminate back-and-forth, not specs that document decisions I have already made. Engineers at ABB told me my specs were unusually complete; this process is why.
What I would not use AI for
Product decisions. Prioritization calls. Discovery synthesis. Those require judgment formed from talking to users and reading data — AI accelerates the inputs, it does not replace the reasoning. I am wary of PMs who use AI to generate roadmaps. I use it to pressure-test the ones I have already formed.

The challenge was identical in kind to Hilo's engagement problem: how do you make a model output feel credible and actionable to a non-technical user? Trust is the product, not the algorithm.
