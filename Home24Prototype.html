What I Found When I Started
Soulspiti, Jan 2024 — artisan D2C marketplace, handcrafted home goods, India
📉

Repeat purchase rate stuck at 8%

Customers bought once and did not return. There was no retention layer — no lifecycle emails, no win-back logic, no referral mechanism. Every customer acquired was effectively a one-time transaction, and the unit economics only worked if that changed.

📱

Mobile was the biggest gap in the funnel

72% of traffic was arriving on mobile. Conversion rate on that traffic: 0.4%. The experience had never been designed as a first-class mobile surface. Every future retention investment would land in a broken environment unless this was fixed first.

🛒

High cart abandonment, no recovery

No cart recovery flow existed. Customers were adding items and leaving with no follow-up. In a high-AOV category where purchase decisions take time, this was a straightforward gap with measurable upside.

🎫

Hidden demand invisible in quantitative data

The funnel metrics pointed to conversion problems, but not their root causes. A manual pass through support tickets revealed a pattern that the dashboards did not show: 36% of customer inquiries contained requests for product customization — a product gap driving abandonment that no standard analytics would surface.

Why this is relevant to home24: Home and living is structurally similar to artisan goods — considered purchase, high AOV, long inter-purchase windows. The retention and CRO challenges I worked on at Soulspiti are the same category of problem this role is designed to solve, at greater scale.

Framing the Problem Before Jumping to Solutions

Before building anything, I needed to understand which problems were structural versus symptoms, and what sequencing made sense. Getting this wrong would mean building retention campaigns that land in a broken mobile environment, or investing in lifecycle emails before fixing the funnel leaks they would fall into.

How I Framed the Problem
Three distinct layers, each requiring a different type of intervention
1
Layer 1 — The surface was broken before retention could work

The mobile conversion problem was upstream of everything else. Any lifecycle email, any win-back campaign, any referral link would land on a mobile experience converting at 0.4%. The return on retention investment was being capped by a product problem that had nothing to do with messaging or timing.

  • 72% of traffic arriving at 0.4% mobile conversion = structural ceiling on all retention work
  • Decision: fix mobile first, before building any lifecycle layer on top of it
  • This meant a deliberate delay on retention feature work — the right call, not the easy one
2
Layer 2 — The funnel had known leaks that A/B testing alone would not close

Conversion analysis showed specific drop-off points: broken search and filtering making product discovery frustrating, and cart abandonment with zero recovery mechanism. These were product gaps, not messaging gaps. Running lifecycle emails on top of a leaking funnel would not move the numbers.

  • Fixed search and filtering as a prerequisite to CRO experimentation
  • Launched cart recovery (3-touch sequence) to capture high-intent abandonment
  • Ran A/B testing programme only once the surface was stable enough to generate valid signal
3
Layer 3 — Retention required a built-from-scratch lifecycle layer

With the surface fixed and funnel leaks addressed, the retention problem was clear: there was no lifecycle layer at all. No welcome series, no post-purchase flow, no win-back, no referral mechanism. Every repeat purchase was happening despite the product, not because of it. This needed to be built end to end.

  • No existing CRM infrastructure — needed to design, sequence, and ship from zero
  • Low-frequency purchase category meant standard win-back timing (30-day) would not apply
  • Referral was the highest-leverage acquisition channel that did not yet exist in any form
The framing principle: In a considered-purchase category, retention investment has a ceiling set by the product surface it lands on. The sequence — fix the surface, close the funnel leaks, then build the retention layer — determined what got built and when.

What I Built

Three sequential workstreams, each dependent on the one before it. Mobile rebuild first. Funnel fixes and CRO programme second. Retention layer third. Every decision was made to compound — not to deliver a quick metric and move on.

Workstream 1 — Mobile Rebuild
Ground-up rebuild, not a responsive fix
A
Decision: full rebuild over incremental improvement

The diagnostic was clear: 72% of traffic at 0.4% conversion was not a UI polish problem. The mobile experience had been built as a desktop-responsive fallback, not as a first-class product surface. Incremental fixes would not close a gap that large. I led a ground-up mobile rebuild — new navigation, product discovery, checkout flow, and onboarding — designed for mobile-first interaction.

  • Rebuilt product discovery: category browsing, search, filtering optimized for mobile
  • Rebuilt checkout: simplified flow with progress indicator, optimized for small screen
  • Result: mobile conversion from 0.4% to 1.1% within 2 weeks of launch (+175%)
Workstream 2 — CRO and Experimentation Programme
Systematic, not ad hoc
B
Structured A/B testing programme across the funnel

Once the mobile surface was stable, I ran a structured A/B testing programme across the full funnel — not single experiments but a compounding programme with documented learnings applied to successive tests. Fixed broken search and filtering. Launched cart recovery. Iterated on product page layout, checkout copy, and offer mechanics.

  • Launched cart recovery (3-touch sequence): 15–20% recovery rate
  • Fixed search and filtering — measurable drop in bounce rate at product discovery stage
  • Compounding A/B programme: overall site conversion 0.67% to 1.92% (+187%) over 27 months
C
Signal-driven feature discovery: customization

The customization feature did not come from a roadmap assumption — it came from a manual pass through support tickets. 36% of customer inquiries contained requests for product customization. That signal was invisible in the quantitative funnel data. I scoped and shipped the feature end to end.

  • Source: support ticket analysis — not dashboards, not surveys
  • 28% customer adoption post-launch
  • Measurable uplift in average order value
Workstream 3 — Retention Layer
Built from zero, designed to compound
D
Lifecycle emails, win-back flows, referral programme

Built the full retention layer from zero: welcome series, post-purchase flows, win-back campaigns, and a referral programme. The referral programme was designed to activate at the post-purchase moment — highest loyalty intent — and it grew into the second-largest acquisition channel.

  • Welcome series, post-purchase flow, win-back campaigns: all designed and shipped from scratch
  • Referral programme: became a standalone acquisition channel
  • Repeat purchase rate: 8% to 16.5% over 27 months
  • Organic traffic: 3.5x growth over the same period

The Crisis Test
Diwali 2024 — the system failed, then got rebuilt
🔥

The failure

Diwali 2024: 287 orders against a system built for 125. 25% cancellation rate. Viral complaints. Everything the retention programme had built was at risk of being undone in a single peak window. This was the highest-stakes moment of the 27-month build.

🔍

The post-mortem

Ran a structured post-mortem with the full team. Root cause: no capacity signal feeding into order intake, no quality threshold triggers. The system had been optimized for conversion but had no protection mechanism when volume exceeded operational capacity.

🛡

The redesign

Shipped an automated circuit breaker system throttling order intake based on real-time capacity and quality signals. Diwali 2025: cancellation held at 6.4% on 120% higher revenue than the prior year on the same peak window.
