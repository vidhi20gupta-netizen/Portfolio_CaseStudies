From Documentation to Developer Activation:
Growing the TB.IO Ecosystem

Vidhi Gupta — Senior PM Ecosystem candidate

Company: Tradebyte Software GmbH (Zalando Group) Product: TB.IO — open developer platform on top of TB.One Note: Speculative case study based on publicly available information

What Tradebyte does and where TB.IO fits

Tradebyte's core product — TB.One — connects fashion and lifestyle brands to marketplaces. A brand signs up, maps its product data, and can sell on Zalando, Amazon, Otto, and 90+ other channels through one platform. Tradebyte has over 1,000 brand customers and 90+ marketplace connections.

TB.IO is the open platform layer on top of TB.One. Launched in April 2021, its goal is to let third-party developers — ERP providers, PIM vendors, logistics tools, shop systems — build their own connectors and apps that work inside TB.One. Instead of Tradebyte building every integration itself, partners build them.

The TB.IO App Store currently lists around 12 certified connectors: Shopify, Akeneo, JTL, Plentymarkets, Actindo, Sage 100, WooCommerce, and a few others.

The Senior PM Ecosystem role

This role owns the developer platform end-to-end: the roadmap, the developer experience, the App Store, the partner relationships, and the metrics that show whether the ecosystem is growing. The squad is small — PM, Engineering Manager, and engineers — but the scope is company-wide. Every brand customer is a potential beneficiary of each new certified connector.

The problem

Core problem: TB.IO has the structural assets of an open platform — public APIs, documentation, an App Store — but the ecosystem is not growing at the pace the platform's scale should enable. Four years after launch, there are roughly 12 certified connectors for 1,000+ brand customers.

Why this matters to Tradebyte's business

Tradebyte's core value to brand customers is reducing time-to-online across marketplaces. Every certified connector in the App Store reduces that time for brands using those tools. A stagnant App Store means brands that use Salesforce Commerce, SAP, or a niche ERP have to build their own custom integration — or wait for Tradebyte to build it. That is friction that can slow deal cycles and increase churn.

What the problem is not

The API works. The documentation exists. Tradebyte is not starting from zero. The problem is specifically that third-party developers are not activating and building certified connectors at the rate you would expect given the size of the customer base.

Two things I do not know yet

This case study is based on public information. Before going further in a real role, I would need to understand:

  • How many developers have registered on TB.IO but not completed a certified connector?
  • What does Tradebyte's internal data show as the top-requested integrations from brand customers?

Both of those answers would change the prioritization significantly.

How I would investigate this

Before proposing any solution, I would run discovery across three areas. My goal is to understand where and why developers are dropping off.

Area 2 — What brand customers are actually asking for

My hypothesis: The demand signal already exists inside Tradebyte — in Success Manager notes and support tickets — but it is not visible to developers. A developer considering whether to build a Salesforce Commerce connector has no way to see that 40 brand customers have asked for it.

FATCHIP was the first certified partner on TB.IO and helped design it. I would interview them and 3–4 other certified partners and ask one question: if you were onboarding a developer to TB.IO today from scratch, what would you change about the process?

I would also talk to 3–5 developers who started a connector but did not finish — the drop-off data matters more than the success stories.

What I would build, and in what order

Based on the discovery above, my working hypothesis is that the problem has three layers, each needing a different fix. I would sequence them by impact and by how much we already know.

First · Months 1–3

Make it easier to get started

The biggest drop-off is probably in the first 24–48 hours. A developer who cannot make a successful API call quickly will move on. Before any ecosystem strategy, I would fix the onboarding experience:

  • A "get started in 60 minutes" guide with working sandbox credentials and a sample connector for one real use case
  • Certification criteria published upfront — effort, timeline, and what success looks like — so developers can commit with confidence
  • A clear commercial model: how connectors are listed, how brand customers find them, what commercial arrangement is possible

None of this requires engineering. It is product and documentation work. But it unblocks everything else.

Second · Months 4–9

Show developers where the demand is

The single highest-leverage change I would make: surface the demand signal that already exists inside Tradebyte. A "most requested integrations" board on TB.IO, updated from Success Manager data, would give developers proof that their work has a ready market before they start building.

  • A public list of the top-requested integrations, with a rough indication of how many brands have asked (even as a range like "10–20 brands")
  • A developer-facing view of App Store connector usage — how many brands have installed each certified connector — so developers can see that adoption actually happens
Third · Months 10–18

Build the flywheel

Once activation and demand signal are working, the ecosystem can start to compound. More certified connectors reduce time-to-online for brands, which is Tradebyte's core commercial value, which makes the platform more attractive to new brands, which creates more demand for connectors.

  • A developer partner program with clear tiers — what you get for being certified, and what you get for hitting adoption milestones
  • An annual ecosystem report: how many developers, how many connectors, how much time-to-online brands are saving. This signals to larger system integrators that the ecosystem is real and worth investing in.
  • AI-assisted connector scaffolding — starter code templates that reduce the time to first working connector from weeks to days

What I would not do

I would not optimize for connector count as a number. Ten well-adopted connectors are worth more than fifty that nobody uses. Every prioritization decision would be anchored to: does this reduce time-to-online for brand customers?
