The trust model — how Freshflow earns the right to be accepted

Every time you accept a recommendation without changing it, the model logs it as a trust signal. Every time you override it, the model logs the gap. Over time, it learns the specific patterns of this store, this category manager, and this customer base — which is different from the store two blocks away.

The goal is not 100% acceptance. The goal is that when the model says high confidence, you trust it. When it says low confidence, you know to look twice. That is the product.

From Spoilage Guesswork to Demand-Signal Ordering

A product approach for AI-powered fresh produce replenishment — Vidhi Gupta, April 2026

The Problem

🗑
What is going wrong today

Category managers order fresh produce using gut instinct, last week's sales data, and manual weather guesses. The result is over-ordering (food in the bin) and under-ordering (empty shelves Thursday afternoon). Neither is a data problem — the right signals exist. It is a product problem: those signals are not connected into a decision the category manager can act on in the 90 seconds they have.

🎯
What the product needs to do

Surface the right recommendation — with the right signals — at the exact moment of ordering. Not a dashboard of charts. Not a weekly report. A single, explainable suggestion per SKU that the category manager can accept, adjust, or skip. The product earns trust through consistency, not features.

Three things I would learn before writing a spec

👀
Watch, don't ask

I would spend time in stores watching category managers order. Not asking what they want — watching where they hesitate, what they check twice, where they override the system they already use.

🗺
Map the workarounds

Every workaround in a manual process is a feature request in disguise. The sticky note on the screen, the personal spreadsheet, the phone call to the supplier — these are the product roadmap.

📞
Sit with Customer Success

The most common reasons clients override recommendations today are the product's biggest gaps. CS knows these patterns. I would treat CS as a discovery team, not a support function.

Product roadmap — three phases
Phase 1
Baseline recommendation layer
Weeks 1–8
Sit on top of the existing workflow. One suggested order quantity per SKU, plus the three signals behind it. No new process. Log every accept and override.
Phase 2
Confidence scoring and explainability
Weeks 8–16
Use override data to find where the model loses trust. Add a simple confidence signal. Give the category manager a mental model for when to trust and when to check.
Phase 3
Closed-loop learning and supplier signals
Weeks 16–26
Formalize the feedback loop. Pull in supplier-side signals: lead times, availability, promotions. This is where Freshflow's position between retailer and supplier becomes a product advantage.

Why this problem

I have built systems that manage perishable supply under demand uncertainty. The operational mechanics at Soulspiti — real-time capacity signals, automated circuit breakers, supplier performance tiers — are a smaller version of what Freshflow solves for retailers. The difference is that Freshflow operates at a scale where the environmental impact is direct and measurable. That is not a line for a cover letter. It is the reason I applied.
